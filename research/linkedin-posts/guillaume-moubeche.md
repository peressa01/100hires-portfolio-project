## Post 3 — Why Anthropic is the best AI investment
**Date:** recent

I've invested millions of $ in secondaries and I've never been more bullish than I am right now on Anthropic.

The numbers:
- Anthropic went from $1B in annualized revenue in December 2024 to $10B by December 2025.
- 10x growth, three years in a row.
- Now crossed $30B (on track to 10x again this year).

No enterprise software company has ever scaled at this rate at this magnitude.

Compare that to OpenAI: just closed at an $852B valuation after raising $122B, still burning cash, not yet profitable.

Anthropic crossed OpenAI in revenue. Has a stronger enterprise mix with 80% of revenue from enterprises, not consumers. Faster path to profitability.

Here's what most people miss: OpenAI is a consumer story. 900M ChatGPT users is impressive. But consumer is volatile, commoditized, and hard to monetize at margin. Anthropic is an enterprise story.

## Post 4 — Jason Cohen built 2 unicorns with opposite playbooks
**Date:** recent

Most founders pick a lane. Bootstrap or raise. Stay in control or go big. Be the King or get Rich.

Jason Cohen picked both. And built 2 unicorns to prove it.

First, he bootstrapped WP Engine to millions in profit with zero funding. Sold it in 2007. Unicorn status by 2009.

Then he did it again but raised $250M from Silver Lake. By 2024: $400M revenue, 200,000 customers.

Two unicorns. Two completely opposite playbooks.

We talk a lot about how to grow a company but no one really talks about the behind the scenes. How do you know if you're a great CEO — or even the right CEO at different stages? It takes a lot of self-awareness to step down from the CEO role.

## Post 5 — From $0 to $150M valuation: wealth management lessons
**Date:** recent

I was raised thinking that money was something you worked for, not something that worked for you.

My grandfather immigrated to France with empty pockets. My parents never went to university.

So when I grew lemlist from 0 to a $150M valuation and made a life-changing amount of money, I discovered a completely different problem.

Building a company is hard. But the rules are legible: work hard, build something people want, don't run out of money.

Wealth management has no legible rules. It has a language designed to confuse you, a cast of characters whose incentives are misaligned with yours, and a long history of extracting money from people who don't know what they don't know.

The first time my private bank called me after I had money, they arrived with products. Complex ones. Structured notes. Multi-layered funds. Things that took hours and multiple spreadsheets to understand. That complexity wasn't sophistication. It was camouflage.
