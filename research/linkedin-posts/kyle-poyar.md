## Post 1 — GTM operators using Claude Code
**Date:** recent

NEW: GTM operators without formal coding skills are now among the most enthusiastic Claude Code users out there.

I set out to survey 200 GTM operators about how they're using Claude Code and Cowork alongside my GTM bestie Maja Voje of Go-To-Market Strategist.

For perhaps the first time in AI, users aren't just saving time. 67% said these products enabled something previously (genuinely) impossible.

What stood out to me from the data:

1. GTM operators split almost evenly between Chat (30%), Code (31%), and Cowork (32%) as their primary Claude product.
2. The average user has adopted 3.5 different use cases — content creation and GTM engines are the two biggest.
3. 55% have already replaced a tool or vendor — usually ChatGPT, an agency, or a contractor.
4. The most common GTM workflows: social posts, building landing pages, SDR tooling, and signal-based outbound sequences.
5. The biggest complaint: credits. Half said running out of tokens mid-workflow is their #1 frustration.

## Post 2 — AI-native CRM vs legacy SaaS
**Date:** recent

SaaS stocks are down 20% this year according to Redpoint. It's the worst performing sector in the S&P 500. The median public SaaS company now trades at a 4.1x forward multiple — the lowest of the past decade.

CRM (the OG of SaaS) is the perfect case study for why SaaS suddenly feels a lot like print newspapers in the early 00s.

An AI-native CRM isn't a regular CRM with a prompt bar. It's a system of intelligence + execution that works with you. The difference:

- Old CRM: Takes weeks or months, highly manual, starts empty
- AI-native CRM: Connect email and it assembles itself from your actual data

## Post 3 — Salesforce going "headless"
**Date:** recent

Salesforce going 'headless' is just the latest in a bigger trend: the SaaS empire (finally) strikes back.

Marc Benioff announced that all of Salesforce & Slack are now exposed to AI agents, meaning you don't need to buy a seat to access Salesforce. They're 'ripping off the band-aid' — betting the long-term value from optimizing for AI agents is >>> near-term losses in seat licenses.

SaaS stocks are down 20% in 2026. Public SaaS companies now trade at a measly 4.1x revenue. SaaS is getting compared to print newspapers.

But SaaS companies have advantages they can exploit in this AI era:
1. It's extremely onerous to build and maintain homegrown enterprise software, even if Claude Code makes it technically feasible.
2. Systems of record with years of proprietary data are extremely hard to replicate.

## Post 4 — Growth endurance in SaaS down to 43%
**Date:** recent

New data: Growth endurance in software is down to only 43%. This is a wake-up call for SaaS.

- Fewer than 1-in-5 startups maintained or improved their growth rate from 2024 to 2025.
- A startup growing between 25-49% in 2024 had a 1-in-25 chance of accelerating to 100%+ growth in 2025.

The startups that inflected their growth did 3 things differently:
1. Reset GTM velocity and increased the pace
2. Shifted from one-off marketing tactics to recurring campaigns
3. Took a different approach to product-led growth

## Post 5 — AI companies going $1M to $10M ARR in 12 months
**Date:** recent

It used to take IPO-caliber SaaS companies >24 months to go from $1M to $10M ARR. AI companies like Clay, Gamma, HeyGen & Fyxer are doing it in 12 months or less.

Over the past two months I've interviewed more than a dozen founders from breakout AI-native companies. The playbook they used:

**A. Plan for a (way) higher bar**
- The typical AI-native company grew from $1M to $10M ARR in just 9 months. Nearly all did it in under 12 months.
- AI companies waited until $2M-$5M ARR to hire first AEs (vs SaaS at $1M ARR). Benefit: didn't blindly follow old SaaS sales playbooks.

**B. Measure the right leading indicators**
- If you reverse engineer going from $1M to $10M ARR in 12 months, everything in GTM needs to move faster. Sales and proof of concept (POC) velocity metrics become critical.
