## Post 1 — Your customers would've paid more
**Date:** recent

Your customers would've paid more.

I asked 50 VPs of Product, CMOs and CEOs what their biggest pricing mistake was.

34% of them said the same thing: we priced too low.

Not complexity. Not bad discounting. Not the wrong model. Underpricing. And waiting too long to fix it.

A CEO: "Had we started higher we would have grown faster. The market and customers would have borne it."

A VP at a $200M+ company: "Rushing to market with transparent pricing that was too low, which resulted in leaving money on the table. We underestimated WTP and it was really hard to walk that back."

A CPO on enterprise deals: "Not pricing for enterprise buyers high enough, thinking we didn't provide enough value. Fixing that allowed us to both increase price and convert at a much higher and faster rate."

Read that last one twice. They raised prices AND converted faster.

## Post 2 — "Contact sales" is a confession
**Date:** recent

"Contact sales" is a confession.

I asked 50 VPs of Product, CMOs and CEOs at B2B SaaS companies one question: what does your pricing page do?

- 27 of them hide pricing entirely.
- 14 show SMB tiers but gate enterprise.
- Only 9 are fully transparent.

The reasons for hiding are the same three, over and over:
- "Competitors would use it against us."
- "Our pricing is too complex to publish."
- "We discount so much, list price is meaningless."

Every one of these is a tell.

If competitors can weaponize your pricing, your positioning isn't strong enough to justify it.
If your pricing is "too complex to publish," your buyer thinks it's complex too. That's how deals die in procurement.
If you discount so heavily that list price is fiction, you're training every AE to cave and every buyer to push harder.

## Post 3 — Most B2B SaaS is guessing at price
**Date:** recent

Most B2B SaaS is guessing at price.

I asked 50 VPs and CEOs how they test pricing before rolling it out.

Only 18% do anything rigorous. A/B tests, conjoint analysis, willingness-to-pay studies.

Actual breakdown:
- 34% "tested" by asking a few friendly customers what they thought
- 28% never tested at all
- 20% ran live sales experiments
- 10% run A/B tests on the pricing page
- 8% do formal conjoint or WTP research

One VP: "I've always been skeptical about how one would even do that, given that asking customers what they want to pay doesn't work."

She's right that asking "what would you pay?" is useless. But conjoint, anchored cohort tests, Van Westendorp — these exist. They work. They're within reach of any company over $10M ARR.

## Post 4 — Nobody owns pricing in B2B SaaS
**Date:** recent

Nobody owns pricing in B2B SaaS.

I asked 50 VPs and CEOs: who owns pricing decisions at your company?

- 38% said "cross-functional committee"
- 20% said "Other" (which meant things like: "a mix of product marketing, CRO and CFO")
- CEO 16%, VP Product 14%, CFO 12%

58% of B2B SaaS companies have no single human accountable for pricing.

Which is why:
- 54% have never formally tested price
- Most pricing pages are gated because "it's complicated"
- The #1 mistake named: "we underpriced and waited too long to fix it"

When pricing is everyone's job, it's nobody's job. Name the owner.

## Post 5 — AI is eating your pricing model
**Date:** recent

AI is eating your pricing model.

54% of B2B SaaS companies repriced in the last 12 months.

I surveyed 50 VPs of Product, Marketing, and CEOs on what prompted their last pricing change.

The top trigger wasn't competition. Wasn't new features. Wasn't customer churn. It was AI.

28% said their last repricing was driven by AI — token costs, credits, outcome-based models, figuring out how to monetize agents.

Compare that to: Market/competitive shifts (20%), New features (18%), Customer friction (16%), Bundling (14%).

Everyone's rewriting pricing right now. Almost nobody knows what they're doing.
